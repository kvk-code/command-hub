## Section 1 — The Five-Day Gap: Durability and the Series Model

**1.1 🔴** The analysis series ran without a documented break from February 22 through April 2, 2026 — 41 consecutive days. Days 42 through 45 (April 3, 4, 5, 6) produced no numbered analysis. This is the first multi-day gap in the series. A series that defines itself by regularity but has no stated frequency requirement or continuity guarantee is not a series by design — it is a series by habit. Habits are fragile. The gap does not disqualify prior analyses, but it removes the tacit claim that the series is self-sustaining. The series has now demonstrated a failure mode it had not previously exhibited.

**1.2 🟡** The gap exists despite the Priority Fix Register listing Fix #1 (Close PR #1) as requiring "10 seconds" of effort — an action that could have been taken on any of the four missing days. The gap is not explained by scarcity of time or effort. What the gap does reveal is that the analysis series is driven by external scheduling, not internal motivation. The series does not self-trigger. This is consistent with Contradiction #22 (analysis series has no termination condition) — but it now has a complementary finding: the series also has no reliable continuation condition.

**1.3 🟢** Four days of gap is recoverable. The series has sufficient history and established format that resumption is straightforward. However, if the gap represents a new baseline — sporadic rather than daily — the metrics dashboard loses its primary function. Day-over-day tracking of file counts, issue severity, and word ratios is only meaningful if the "day" increment is consistent. A series that runs on unknown intervals cannot produce valid time-series analysis. The dashboard would need to shift from day-based to analysis-number-based framing exclusively.
